Catholic bishops urge Kenyans to take COVID-19 jab, differ with doctors who opposed it

March 2024 · 3 minute read

- The clerics said the remarks made by Catholic doctors who criticised the administration of COVID-19 doses were made in their own capacity

- Bishop Philip Anyolo said the church was ready to support the government in its nationwide vaccination exercise

- The men of God said the health department had already assured Kenyans of safety of AstraZeneca vaccine stating there was nothing to worry about

Kenyan Catholic bishops have urged Kenyans to take the COVID-19 jab despite doctors associated with the church advising against it.

In a statement signed by chairman Philip Anyolo, the clerics said the move by Catholic doctors to criticise the administration of COVID-19 jabs was made in their own capacity and did not reflect the church's position.

Church to support government in vaccination exercise

Bishop Anyolo said the church was ready to support the government in its nationwide vaccination exercise.

"It should be noted that those doctors cannot and should not purport to speak in the name of the Catholic Church."We, the Catholic bishops in Kenya, avail our vast network of healthcare facilities to be utilised to ensure the vaccination is rolled out in an objective and well-coordinated manner," Anyolo said.

The bishops said the health department had already assured Kenyans of the AstraZeneca vaccine's safety, adding there was nothing to worry about.

"We, therefore, appeal to the conscience of Kenyans to accept the COVID-19 vaccines in light of the assurance given by the Ministry of Health," part of the statement read.

The church asked Kenyans to continue adhering to COVID-19 protocols by properly wearing face masks, frequently washing hands and keeping social distance in compliance with the Ministry of Health guidelines.

Catholic doctors say jab unnecessary

The Catholic doctors led by Stephen Karanja had earlier said the COVID-19 vaccine was unnecessary amid plans by the government to roll out phased vaccination.

The health experts said the only ways to prevent the spread of the virus was by wearing masks, mass testing and observation of COVID-19 patients.

"We advise that a COVID-19 vaccine is unnecessary and should not be given. We appeal to all the people of Kenya to avoid taking it," the Catholic health professionals said in a statement.

The Catholic Church has over the years been cautious on vaccination programmes by the government.

In October 2015, the late Eldoret Catholic bishop, Cornelius Korir, raised concerns following the deaths of two babies after a vaccination exercise in Elgeyo Marakwet.

"There is a dire need for questionable vaccines to be tested and results given to the public to assure people of vaccination safety. The continued ignorance by the government is a big concern," Korir said.
