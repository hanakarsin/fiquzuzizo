Kenyan Artistes to Expect Less Royalties as KAMP Says COVID-19 Affected Collections Negatively

February 2024 · 3 minute read
  • The music producers association has alerted Kenyan artistes who are yet to receive peanuts after the body said it collected fewer revenues
  • The Collective Management Organisation(CMO) is yet to distribute KSh 41 million out of the KSh 114 million collected
  • KAMP had been deregistered alongside MCSK and PRISK for mismanaging artistes revenues

Kenyan recording artistes are staring at yet another disappointment after Kenya Association of Music Producers (KAMP) said it will remit less royalties.

KAMP represents the rights and interests of Kenyan producers.

The body cited COVID-19 and a lack of enforcement support from the government as the reason for the drop in revenues. It revealed it collected only KSh 114 million last year.

Out of the KSh 114 million, only KSh 41 million was distributed, the body adding that the amount would still go down due to the costly administrative costs that needed to be catered for.

"We have operational costs to take care of our spending. Meaning the money to go to our artistes and members will be reduced," said Mbugua Njoroge, KAMP CEO.

KAMP also decried the withdrawal of its security agents who had helped in the collection of revenues from business premises.

Initially, the CMO would amass revenues to a high of half a billion before the collection started to dive.

KECOBO deregisters KAMP, MCSK, PRISK

Recently, KAMP was deregistered alongside MCSK and PRISK for failure to account for the royalties owed to artistes.

Kenya Copyright Board (KECOBO) claimed the CMOs had mismanaged the monies collected.

The distribution excluded money received and channelled to other accounts that could not be captured in the KECOBO tracking system.

According to the board's executive officer Edward Sigei, one of the licensing conditions require all the CMOs to distribute 70% of royalties collected over a given period of time.

Consequently, Sigei deregistered the three bodies for violating KECOBO's licensing conditions and regulations.

"The board, after being dissatisfied with the CMO explanation in response to show cause letter, invoked the provisions of the Copyright Act to deregister MCSK, PRISK and KAMP. Following the revocation of licenses, collection of royalties has been suspended for three months or until further advised," Sigei said in a statement on Tuesday, August 24.

Kenyan artistes have been on record complaining about peanuts in form of royalties they have been receiving from the three CMOs.

Leading a host of musicians and other celebrities in an online troll against the CMOs, rapper Khaligraph Jones had taken to Instagram to air out his thoughts.

According to the star, the audit was not necessary since Kenyans have known all along that people have been stealing funds meant for artistes.

''The audit was unnecessary, we have been saying this from the beginning, the Kenyan entertainment industry keeps on suffering, DJs, artistes, event organisers, bouncers, dancers waiters, club owners, restaurants etc,'' he said.
